Research

My research studies the communal foundations of party systems: the population groups on which parties stand, the work parties do to keep those groups politically cohesive, and the conditions under which communal blocs dissolve or prove never to have been as unified as they appeared. A party system, in this view, is an alignment between parties and territorially arranged population groups rather than between parties and individual voters, and the same electorate can sustain one coalition under one spatial arrangement and break it under another. I study how that alignment is built, maintained, and remade, and I approach it from both sides of the relationship.

From the population side, urbanization, migration, and generational replacement continually re-sort the groups that parties depend on. My published work shows that a cleavage that looks decisive on a national map can dissolve once spatial heterogeneity is modeled, and that political machines built on settled populations come apart as those populations move. A central part of my dissertation carries this logic into the contemporary United States, tracing how Latino voters move, cluster, and even change the ethnic and partisan categories they are recorded under.

From the party side, parties do not wait passively for demographic change. They defend and sometimes manufacture the populations that sustain them, through state-directed settlement, mobilizational campaigns, and the management of their own elites, and when these efforts fail, party systems fracture from within. This is why the same agenda that studies voters re-sorting also studies how regimes engineer electoral geographies, how ruling parties keep state bureaucracies subordinate to political ends, and why dominant parties so often manufacture their own splits.

Because the groups that decide elections are frequently the ones surveys measure worst, a methodological strand runs through the whole agenda. I develop Bayesian, simulation-based tools, foremost the Bayesian Approximate Computation with Hierarchical framework (BACH), that recover group political behavior from aggregate election returns when conventional survey-based methods break down. These tools do more than sharpen estimates. They let me ask whether a putative voting bloc is one group or several, and so whether communal cohesion is a real feature of the electorate or an artifact of the categories our data report.

Peer-Reviewed Publications

“Myth and Measurement: Spatial Heterogeneity, Ecological Fallacy, and the Urban Narrative in Peninsular Malaysia’s Electoral Politics.” 2026. Journal of East Asian Studies 26(1): 87–112. [Open Access] [Replication]

Does Malaysia have an urban–rural divide in voting? For a decade, aggregate election returns said yes and surveys said no. I show that both are right: the divide read off the aggregates is largely an artifact of scale, produced by a handful of dense urban centers and uneven urbanization across states, and it nearly disappears once within- and between-state spatial heterogeneity is modeled, leaving ethnicity the dominant axis of competition. The paper combines a within-district design that exploits concurrent federal and state elections with multilevel regression and poststratification (MRP) validated against district returns, and it makes a broader case that aggregate election data, handled carefully, can answer questions where reliable surveys are scarce.

“Social Transformation in Malaysia and the Fragmentation of the Malay-Dominant Political Parties — Based on a Social Cleavage Structure Theory Perspective” (with Congcong Fu). Southeast Asian Studies (东南亚研究) 2021(3): 1–29. [Chinese Full Text] [English Translation]

An early statement of the social-cleavage approach that anchors my later work, tracing how social transformation fragmented Malaysia’s Malay-dominant parties.

“Social Transformation, Dispute over Routes and the Split of Malaysian Islamic Party (PAS)” (with Congcong Fu). Southeast Asian Affairs (南洋问题研究) 2020(1): 79–95. [Chinese Full Text] [English Translation]

A study of the internal route disputes behind the split of the Malaysian Islamic Party, an early case in my work on how dominant and communal parties fracture from within.

Under Review

“Morning News: Public Signals, Elite Defections, and the Collective Abdication of Authoritarian Regimes.”

When an authoritarian regime reaches a survival crisis, its elites face a coordination problem: each may privately judge the leader finished, but moving first alone is ruinous. I argue that a public channel, the morning’s newspaper, is what lets dispersed elites coordinate their abandonment of the leader, because it is a signal every elite reads at once and knows the others are reading. I formalize this as a global game with a “publicity multiplier” and test it against an original day-by-day, elite-by-elite record of stance-taking during the fall of Indonesia’s New Order in 1998, assembled from thousands of photographed newspaper pages, declassified diplomatic cables, and Indonesian-language retrospectives, with seven comparative crises coded on a fixed rubric. Leaders fall where a wide public channel meets a demand that has narrowed onto the leader by name, and survive otherwise at comparable levels of threat.

“Colonial Institutionalization and Unborn Civil Society in Post-Colonial States: Malaysia and Indonesia in Comparative Perspective.”

Why do some communal institutions survive decades of hostile policy while comparable institutions next door are extinguished? Malaysian Chinese-language schools persisted through sixty years of Malay-primacy rule, while New Order Indonesia eliminated Chinese-medium education within a year, even though the same Indonesian state could not contain Islamic mobilization. I argue that the institutional form a communal sphere takes is set by colonial choices to register, codify, or spatially contain it, interacting with the group’s underlying social density, and that reading post-colonial Asia through the European civil-society template has produced decades of misdiagnosis. The argument rests on a paired comparison of Malaysia and Indonesia across two domains, Chinese education and Islamic authority, supported by cross-national civil-society indices and multi-wave survey microdata.

“Symbolic Power and the Functional Divide: Campaigns Across the Party-State Spectrum.”

Are the party and the state the same thing in party-state regimes, and what do mobilizational campaigns do between them? Against the standard reading that campaigns fuse party and state, I argue that a campaign is how a party overrides the bureaucratic state it built, compelling action that routine authority and patronage cannot, and that a regime such as Xi Jinping’s China deliberately keeps this capacity alive rather than completing the transition to ordinary administration. The paper treats party-state fusion as a graded spectrum rather than a communist endpoint, and tests the argument with original data spanning seven decades of the Chinese party press, an event study of firms’ market reactions to political campaigns, and daily social-media discourse during a major mobilization.

Working Papers

“What Race and Ethnicity Hide: Bayesian Approximate Computation with Hierarchical Framework (BACH) for Subgroup Voting Behavior under Partisan Polarization, Cross-Cutting Cleavages, and Survey Nonresponse.” [SSRN]

Knowing how demographic groups vote is foundational to political science, yet the surveys we rely on to measure it are failing, and no reweighting recipe removes the bias. The discipline increasingly turns to ecological inference (EI), recovering group behavior from aggregates such as precinct returns and census tables, but the standard estimators assume an electorate that no longer exists: one whose groups are internally uniform and whose survey nonrespondents resemble respondents. BACH is a simulation-based estimator that withstands the three forces breaking those assumptions at once, namely partisan polarization, nonresponse correlated with vote choice, and widening cleavages within racial groups. It draws subgroup vote probabilities from survey-calibrated priors, simulates the returns they would produce, and keeps the draws consistent with observed returns, reporting honest uncertainty under partial identification. Validated against the leading EI and MRP estimators and against a statewide voter file of millions of registrants, BACH matches the best methods on accuracy while dominating them on calibration, and in replications of published studies it recovers within-group contrasts the originals discarded as noise: in one, national origin explains more variation in Hispanic vote choice than education does. The benchmark runs in two stages on high-performance computing: a first-stage factorial grid under Missing At Random (MAR) nonresponse, then a six-factor expansion of 9,375 conditions into the harder Not Missing At Random (NMAR) regime, where whether a person responds depends on how they would vote.

“Research Note: A Higher-Order Method of Moments Approach to Ecological Inference under Extreme Survey Nonresponse.” [SSRN]

A companion to BACH for the hardest case, where surveys are not merely biased but unusable. I show that the higher-order moments of the cross-unit distribution of aggregate election outcomes carry enough information to identify group-level voting rates from public data alone, with survey information entering only as a soft, down-weighted constraint. The estimator is aimed at historical elections and democracies without reliable polling infrastructure, extending the agenda beyond the United States.

“Bridges Burnt: Uneven Urbanization and the Spatial Breakdown of Patronage Infrastructure in Malaysia.” [Latest Draft]

Why do dominant parties decline gradually and unevenly, losing some strongholds decades before others? I argue that patronage is not a fungible exchange but a delivery technology that depends on geographic social closure, so urbanization dissolves it from the outside in. In Malaysia, colonial and postcolonial policy placed Chinese New Villages near towns and Malay land-settlement schemes deep in the interior, so the ruling coalition’s own developmental success dismantled its electoral machine settlement type by settlement type, an “authoritarian urbanization dilemma” it could not spend its way out of. The argument is tested on a constituency-level panel spanning eleven Malaysian general elections with ecological inference, an event-study design, distributional analysis, and survey-based mediation, and it carries comparative scope conditions for Singapore, Taiwan, Japan, and Indonesia.

“Crisis from Within: Elite Defection and Intraparty Competition in Dominant-Party Regimes.”

Why do elites defect from dominant parties at the height of the party’s strength, and why do most breakaway parties fail while a few endure? Because such parties monopolize access to office, losing an internal contest turns a routine setback into a forced choice between marginalization and exit, so defection is driven by intraparty competition rather than by voter signals or economic decline. Breakaway parties then survive only when they attach to a social cleavage the dominant party cannot absorb, which ties party fragmentation back to the geography of population groups. The evidence is a set of candidate-level career panels I built from primary electoral records for every United Malays National Organisation (UMNO), Liberal Democratic Party (LDP), and Kuomintang (KMT) candidate across each party’s dominant era, analyzed with conditional logit, a regression-discontinuity test, causal mediation, and a cross-national probe.

“Inducing Measurement Error? Fixed Effect under Asymptotic Conditions” (with Robert Franzese).

When does the two-way fixed-effects specification widely used in panel research remove bias, and when does it induce the very measurement error it is meant to clean? With Robert Franzese, I study the asymptotic conditions that separate the two.

“Plant Closures and Trump-Related Twitter Discussion: A Spatial-Temporal Description” (with Robert Franzese). [Latest Draft]

With Robert Franzese, I trace how local plant-closing shocks reshaped Trump-related and far-right engagement on social media, and how that discourse diffused across space and time, using a national geocoded corpus of social-media activity around the closings.

Work in Progress

My dissertation develops the communal-foundations argument across the United States, Malaysia, Taiwan, and Japan. Its United States component uses two decades of statewide voter-registration records to study how Latino voters move and cluster, and how migration into new local contexts reshapes the bundle of ethnic identification and party registration, including cases where the recorded ethnic category itself changes. A paired Malaysia–Taiwan study asks why rapid urbanization has not eroded ethnic voting, locating the answer in urban ethnic enclaves that preserve segregation and co-ethnic exchange inside growing cities.
